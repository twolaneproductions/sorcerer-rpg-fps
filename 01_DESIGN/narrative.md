# Summary

Following in the footsteps of your absent master, you are a sorcerer apprentice who ventures to a small kingdom plagued by monsters, demons, and the undead. Tasked by the lord of the realm to defeat the banished lord, you set off through the woods to discover into the castle ruins in the hope that you can put an end to whatever devilry is afoot.

# Setting

## **THORNWALL** — The Town at the Edge of the Woods

Thornwall takes its name from the dense hedge of blackthorn that the townspeople have reinforced along the outer perimeter of their wooden palisade wall — a desperate addition made in recent months as the woods began to change. Torches burn along the wall at all hours now. Nobody sleeps well. The town is not dying but it is holding its breath.

The streets are cobblestone in the center, mud at the edges. The buildings lean slightly toward each other across the narrow lanes as if for comfort. Smoke from cookfires hangs low. Dogs don't bark here anymore — they whimper and stay close to doorways. Children are kept indoors after midday.

### The Gates and the Wall

The main gate is manned by four guards at all times — double the usual complement, recently instated by the Captain of the Watch after a farmer was found at the treeline, alive but blank-eyed and muttering in no known language. The guards are visibly frightened beneath their professionalism. One of them, young and red-haired, will speak candidly if the player lingers — he saw something move in the woods three nights ago that walked like a man but had too many joints in its legs. His older partner tells him to shut up every time he brings it up.

A crude shrine has been erected just inside the gate — candles, dried flowers, small carved figures. It is tended by whoever passes. Nobody organized it. It simply appeared one morning and people keep adding to it.

### Important Sites

**The Hammer and Song — Blacksmith**
Run by **Aldra**, a broad-shouldered woman in her fifties with a long silver braid and permanently scorched forearms. She is the most practically-minded person in Thornwall and barely conceals her contempt for the town's collective panic. Her forge is the warmest, loudest place in town — a deliberate psychological anchor for the community. She works longer hours than she needs to, partly to keep busy, partly because the sound of the hammer seems to keep people calm. She sells standard equipment but has recently begun experimenting with etched protective runes on her metalwork at the suggestion of the town's hedge-witch, about whom she is professionally skeptical and personally fond. She will upgrade the player's basic equipment and makes an offhand remark about the old wizard who used to stop by — *odd fellow, never caused a lick of trouble, didn't deserve what the townsfolk did to him.*

**The Green Mortar — Apothecary**
A cramped, aromatic shop run by **Brother Oswin**, a former monk who left his order under circumstances he declines to discuss. Shelves from floor to ceiling, bundles of dried herbs hanging from every beam, dozens of tiny labeled drawers behind the counter. He is gentle, precise, and quietly terrified — three of his regular herb gatherers have stopped going into the woods, which means his supply of several key ingredients is running low. He sells Tinctures of Mending, Bloodmoss Compress, and basic Mana Vials but warns the player he can't promise restocking. He is one of the townspeople who will speak warmly and at length about the old wizard, whom he calls *the most knowledgeable herbalist I ever met, and I trained with monks for eleven years.* He will mention the relic obliquely — that the priest has something of the old man's and has been praying over it nightly.

**The Crooked Candle — Hedge-Witch's Workshop**
Set slightly apart from the main lane, down a narrow alley that smells of sulfur and dried roses, **Maret** operates out of a building that appears to be three structures that have grown into each other over decades. She is ageless in the way that makes people uneasy — she could be forty or seventy and carries herself like neither. Her fingers are permanently ink-stained and she wears seven rings, none of them decorative. She is the source of the town's more exotic consumables — Ward Stones, Smoke of Confusion, Sorcerer's Salve — and will trade in information as readily as goods. She knows about the wraith. She will not say so directly but if the player asks about the ruins she goes very still for a moment before answering. She is the one who will hint most directly that something of the old wizard's was left behind, and that the pool near the ruins *is not a natural pool.* She sells the Warded Travel Cloak and the Spellbinder Gloves and is the only person in town who treats the player as a fellow professional rather than a curiosity.

**The Church of the Pale Candle**
The largest stone building in Thornwall, old enough that its foundations predate the town around it. **Father Cassian** is a man of perhaps sixty, lean and white-haired, with the careful diction of someone who chooses every word before releasing it. He is not a fearful man but he is a troubled one. The church has been full every night for three weeks — people sleeping on the pews rather than in their homes, which distresses him and moves him in equal measure. He has the relic locked in the vestry and prays over it genuinely, not superstitiously — he believes it belongs to a good man and that keeping it safe is a form of honoring that goodness. When the player speaks to him about the wizard he is the one who tells the story most completely and most honestly, including the town's shame at driving him off. He will give up the relic without resistance once the player's purpose is clear — he has been waiting, he says, for someone to come along who would know what to do with it.

**The Broken Antler — Tavern**
The social and informational heart of Thornwall. Run by **Hobb**, a heavyset man with a remarkable mustache and the practiced calm of someone who has decided that panic is bad for business and therefore refuses to indulge it. The tavern is full even in the daytime now — people who don't want to be alone at home. The conversations the player can overhear or join here are the primary source of town rumors: fragments about the wraith, about the woods, about the couple who barely made it back last week from the northern road, about a merchant who swears he saw the Banished Lord's standard flying from the castle ruins three months ago. A retired soldier sits alone in the corner every night nursing the same drink and will tell the player, if approached carefully, that he served in the castle guard before the fall. He knows the layout of the ruins from memory. He will sketch it on a napkin if the player earns his trust, providing a rough map that proves partially but not entirely accurate — things have changed since he was last inside.

**The Cartwright's Yard — General Supplies**
**Tomlin** the cartwright has pivoted his business in recent weeks from wagon repair to general survival goods — rope, torches, flint, rations, leather straps. He is young, entrepreneurial, and slightly ghoulish in his practicality, having recognized that fear is a seller's market. He is not malicious — he genuinely wishes the town wasn't in danger — but he also has a ledger to balance. He sells the Ghostlight Torch and basic restorative items at slightly inflated prices, and will haggle enthusiastically if the player engages him.

**The Couple's Home**
The pair rescued at the game's opening — **Sela** and **Davan** — live in a modest house near the inner wall. Finding them after escorting them to the gate is optional but rewarding. They will give the player the **Bread and Salt** here, quietly and without ceremony, pressing it into the player's hands before they leave for the ruins. Davan, who says very little throughout, says something here — a single line. Whatever it is, it should land.

### The Mood of the Town

Thornwall is not a ghost town. That is the important thing. People are still buying bread, arguing over fences, falling in love, drinking too much. Life continues because it must. But there is a quality to the light here — the torches burn a little too orange, the shadows gather a little too quickly at the edges of lanes — that tells the player something is seeping in from outside the walls, slowly, like damp through old stone. 

The evil in the woods is not yet inside Thornwall. But it is *learning* the shape of the walls.

## **THORNWALL CASTLE** — Seat of the King

Thornwall Castle rises from the town's northern quarter, sharing the town's name the way a man shares his name with his father's house — the town grew up around it, not the other way around. It is not enormous by the standards of great kingdoms, but it is solid, well-maintained, and unmistakably inhabited by people who still believe in what it represents. Banners fly from the towers. The stonework is clean. The guards stand straight.

This matters because Ashenmoor, glimpsed later through the trees, will register as its dark mirror — same basic architectural bones, entirely different spirit. The player should feel the contrast viscerally.

### Exterior and Approach

**The Castle Approach**
A short cobblestone road leads from Thornwall's main thoroughfare up a gentle rise to the castle's outer gate, passing between two rows of old oak trees that have been there longer than anyone living can account for. Carved into each tree's bark, layered over decades, are protective symbols — some crude, some elegant, each made by a different hand. The wizard made several of them, years ago. Nobody took them down when he was driven off.

**The Outer Gate**
Where Ashenmoor's barbican is narrow and oppressive, Thornwall's outer gate is wide and well-lit, with iron lanterns burning on either side. Two guards stand here — the pair who initially refuse the player entry and require the small demonstration of magic before relenting. They are not villainous, just frightened and rule-bound. The younger one will apologize, afterward, every time the player passes through for the rest of the game.

**The Outer Bailey**
A modest open space between the outer gate and the castle's main body. A training ground occupies the eastern half — currently in use even as the player arrives, soldiers drilling with a focused intensity that speaks to how seriously the castle is taking the threat from the north. A couple of off-duty guards sit on a low wall watching, offering quiet commentary to each other. The western half holds a small stable — real horses, warm-eyed, well-fed, a deliberate contrast to what the player will find later.

### Important Sites Within the Castle

**The Great Hall**
Thornwall's great hall is a working room as much as a ceremonial one — long tables pushed to the sides, a large map table dominating the center, advisors and captains moving around it with the organized urgency of people managing a genuine crisis. Candles burn everywhere. The walls are hung with old battle standards, some of them tattered in ways that suggest genuine history rather than decoration.

The **King's Steward**, a precise and slightly exhausted man named **Aldric**, intercepts the player here before granting access to the throne room. He is not obstructive — just procedural, the human machinery of a functioning court still turning even under pressure. He has the quality of a man who has not slept properly in several weeks and has decided to manage this by becoming more efficient rather than less.

**The Throne Room**
Smaller than the hall — intimate, almost. The throne itself is plain oak with iron fittings, notable for its lack of ostentation. **King Orvyn** sits in it the way a man sits at his own desk — like it is a tool rather than a symbol. He is perhaps sixty, grey-bearded, broad through the shoulders in a way that suggests he came to kingship from soldiering rather than the other way around. He has the directness of someone who has been delivered enough bad news that ceremony around it strikes him as wasteful.

He receives the player without theater. He believes them quickly — not naively, but with the pragmatic recognition that only a sorcerer's apprentice would have gotten through his gates with a simple demonstration. He tells the story of the master's departure north plainly and without embellishment, which makes it land harder. He makes the promise of reward in the same register — not a proclamation, just a statement of intent from a man whose word appears to mean something.

Two things on the throne room walls are worth noting: a portrait of a younger Orvyn standing beside a figure who is clearly the wizard — companion pose, easy body language, old friendship visible in it. And a second portrait, partially covered with a dark cloth, which the player cannot see clearly and which nobody in the castle will discuss.

**The Royal Quarters**
Not accessible to the player directly but visible through a doorway off the throne room corridor — a sitting room where **the Queen** can be briefly glimpsed. She is not introduced formally. She watches the player pass with an expression that is not hostile but deeply searching, as if looking for something specific in their face. She does not speak. She is the second most interesting person in the castle and the player is given almost no access to her, which is the point.

### Castle Personnel

**Captain Amis of the Watch**
The commander of Thornwall Castle's military, encountered in the outer bailey overseeing training. She is forty, compact, practical, wearing her authority the way experienced soldiers do — lightly, because it doesn't require display. She will speak to the player if approached, primarily to extract information — she is less interested in the player's quest than in anything they might have observed about the woods and the outer ruins. She trades information fairly. She knows more about the threat from the north than she has told the King, not from deception but from a soldier's instinct to verify before alarming. She is one of the few people in the castle who does not seem frightened — she seems, instead, like a person who has converted fear into a very long to-do list and is working through it methodically.

**The Court Physician, Galen**
An elderly man found in a small room off the main corridor, surrounded by books and specimens and the organized chaos of a working scholar. He treated the wizard once, years ago, for something minor — a sprained wrist, he says, from moving too many bookshelves alone — and found him to be the most stimulating conversationalist he had encountered in thirty years of court life. He is visibly angry, in the quiet persistent way of old men, that the wizard was driven off. He will give the player a small supply of Tinctures of Mending from his own stores before they leave — not as a transaction but as a gesture. *Take these. Come back.*

**The Young Page, Emmet**
Perhaps twelve, gap-toothed, assigned to run messages between the hall and the throne room and currently doing so with the barely-contained energy of a child who has been told to stay calm during a crisis and finds this instruction physically difficult. He attaches himself briefly to the player with the frank adAmistion of someone who has been surrounded by frightened adults and finds the player's apparent purposefulness exotic and wonderful. He tells the player, in a rush, three things: that the King hasn't laughed since the news came about the north, that Captain Amis sleeps in her armor now, and that he once saw the old wizard turn a sparrow into a small candle flame and back again just to prove he could. He says this last thing with the reverence of someone describing a Amiscle.

### The Castle's Atmosphere

Thornwall Castle is afraid but it is not defeated. That is the crucial distinction. The maps are still being updated. The soldiers are still drilling. The King is still holding audiences. The lanterns are lit and the banners are flying and the cook is still producing three meals a day for the household because the cook has decided, apparently unilaterally, that this is how she will fight the darkness — with hot food served on time.

There is grief here, underneath the function. The wizard's absence is felt as a specific absence rather than a general one — in the portrait on the throne room wall, in Galen's quiet anger, in the protective symbols carved into the oaks that nobody has touched. Something good was lost when the town drove him off, and the people who understood that are still carrying it.

The player leaves Thornwall Castle with a quest, a small supply of healing items, and the faint sense that all of this — the drilling soldiers, the working maps, the cook's hot meals, the page running messages with his gap-toothed intensity — is worth fighting for. Which is precisely what a good inciting location should accomplish.

## **THE WYCHFELD** — The Land Between - Overworld

The locals have always called it the Wychfeld — an old word that means something close to *the field of crooked things*, though most people who use it have forgotten the etymology and simply know it as the name for everything outside the walls. In better times it was farmland and managed forest, threaded with roads and dotted with the homesteads of families who supplied Thornwall with grain, timber, and wool. The roads still exist. The homesteads mostly still stand. The families are gone.

The Wychfeld is perhaps four hours' travel on foot between Thornwall's gate and Ashenmoor's outer ruins — not vast, but large enough to feel like a world of its own, and strange enough that four hours inside it feels considerably longer. Distance behaves oddly here. The sky is always slightly wrong — not dramatically, not in ways that announce themselves, just slightly too dark at midday, slightly too bright at dusk, the sun's position never quite matching the time of day the player's instincts suggest it should be.

This is not wilderness in the romantic sense. It is wilderness in the older sense — a place outside the jurisdiction of human order, where the rules that apply inside walls do not apply, and where something else's rules are slowly being written in their place.

### The Road North

**The King's Road**
The main thoroughfare north from Thornwall is old Imperial stone — broad, well-laid, built to last centuries and having done exactly that. It is the straightest thing in the Wychfeld and in some ways the most unsettling because of it. Something about a straight road through a landscape that has gone crooked emphasizes the crookedness. Crows line the milestones. The drainage ditches on either side run with water that moves in the wrong direction — not dramatically, just persistently, uphill toward the north.

The road is the player's spine through the overworld — departure point and return reference, the thread back to Thornwall. Leaving it feels like a choice. Being forced off it feels like a threat.

Several things on or near the road are worth noting: an abandoned merchant's cart, one wheel still turning slowly in no wind; a milestone with the distances chiseled off, deliberately, recently; and at roughly the halfway point, a crossroads where the intersecting road leads east into deeper forest and west toward the edge of a blighted moor. Both directions contain optional content. Neither direction is safe.

### The Zones

**The Amber Fields**
The first stretch north of Thornwall's gate — former farmland, wide and open, the grass gone yellow-grey and knee-high with disuse. Farmhouses stand at intervals, doors open, interiors visible from the road as the player passes. Everything inside them is exactly where it would have been on a normal morning — a pot still on the fire hook, a child's wooden toy on the floor, a half-written letter on a table. The families left quickly and recently, and did not stop to take anything with them.

This is where the player first encounters the **Demonic Wolves** — not in the forest but here, in the open fields, which is wrong and frightening in the specific way that a predator appearing where it shouldn't be is wrong and frightening. They hunt in loose formations, coordinating in ways wolves don't naturally coordinate. They do not howl. They are absolutely silent until they are very close.

One farmhouse in the Amber Fields is not abandoned. An old woman named **Gest** refused to leave and is still there, barricaded in, entirely unbothered in the way that very old people sometimes achieve a kind of fearlessness through simple familiarity with the worst that can happen. She has been watching the demonic wolves from her window for three weeks. She has notes. She will share them with the player in exchange for company and a small errand — something needed from another farmhouse nearby that she can no longer safely reach herself. Her notes are mechanically useful — they describe the wolves' patrol patterns with the unsentimental precision of a woman who spent sixty years farming and understands animal behavior. She also makes the best cup of tea in the Wychfeld and the player should feel the specific melancholy of leaving her there when the errand is done.

**The Greywood**
The forest begins where the farmland ends, announced by a treeline so abrupt it looks planted — which it was, generations ago, as a managed boundary between agricultural and wild land. Whatever managed it has stopped. The trees press close to the road and their canopy filters the already-wrong light into something greenish and aqueous, like being inside a very large, very old bottle.

The Greywood is the primary combat zone of the overworld and the home of the **Goblins** — not the small comic creatures of lighter fantasy but something more unsettling, roughly human-sized, quick and clever and working in organized squads. They have established camps in the ruins of forest woodcutters' shelters, using the existing structures with a pragmatic intelligence that makes them feel like occupiers rather than monsters. Their camps have command structures. Some of them give orders. The ones giving orders are larger and stay back and are harder to kill and worth killing specifically because of what they drop.

The Greywood contains two significant non-combat locations. The first is a **Woodcutter's Chapel** — a tiny stone structure, barely a room, built at an old crossroads within the forest. It is untouched by the corruption, for reasons nobody in the game explains. Candles inside it are still burning — have apparently been burning without being tended. It functions as a rest point and a place where the player can use the Bloodmoss Compress and Bread and Salt safely, without combat interruption. It feels like a held breath.

The second is a **Ruined Watchtower** on a rise above the forest floor — climbable, offering a view of the full Wychfeld including the distant smear of Ashenmoor on the northern horizon. Reaching it requires a goblin camp clearance. The view from the top is the only moment in the overworld journey where the player sees the full geography at once — Thornwall's lights behind them, Ashenmoor's darkness ahead, the road threading between. It is the game's establishing shot, delivered late, after the player has already walked most of the distance. It recontextualizes the journey.

**The Blighted Moor**
West of the King's Road, accessible via the crossroads at the halfway point, the moor is optional territory — punishing, atmospheric, and rewarding for players who persist through it. The ground is soft and unreliable, the path non-obvious, the light the worst in the entire Wychfeld. Pale, flat, directionless — the kind of light that makes depth perception unreliable.

The moor's primary inhabitants are **Wraiths of a lesser order** than the wizard in the pool — fragments of the dead, not coherent enough to bargain with, only coherent enough to resent the living. They do not move so much as relocate — present, then not present, then present elsewhere, never seen in motion. Fighting them requires the Ghostlight Torch to make them fully solid and targetable, which is both a mechanical requirement and a piece of atmospheric world-building — they are most dangerous in the dark and the moor has a great deal of dark.

At the moor's centre, barely visible until the player is nearly upon it, is a **Sunken Abbey** — half-submerged in the moor's soft ground, listing badly, its bell tower still upright by what appears to be stubbornness rather than structural integrity. Inside it the player finds the game's first **Concentrated Essence** and the **Talisman of the Ancestors** — both valuable enough to justify the detour. They also find, in the abbey's crypt, a series of carved memorial tablets that tell the history of the Wychfeld in fragments — including a reference to the castle before it was Ashenmoor, and a name that matches one of the portraits in Thornwall Castle's throne room. The covered one.

**The Ashenmoor Approach**
The final stretch of the King's Road, north of the Greywood, where the forest thins and the land opens again into something that was once a second ring of farmland around the castle. It is not farmland now. The soil is black and cracked in geometric patterns, as if something beneath it has been drawing heat out of the ground from below. Nothing grows. The farmhouses here did not have time for orderly evacuation — doors hang open on broken hinges, contents disturbed, in one case a table overturned and not righted. The transition from the Greywood into this zone is the overworld's tonal pivot point. The forest was dangerous. This is something different. This is aftermath.

The outer ruins of Ashenmoor are visible from here — and now that the player can see them clearly, the castle's full silhouette registers on the horizon with the oppressive weight of a destination that has been waiting. The dark pool is visible too, just north of the ruins, perfectly still, catching no light at all from the sky above it.

The crows, noted earlier at the outer ruins, are also here in the Ashenmoor Approach. Hundreds of them. Lining the dead field, sitting on the cracked earth, facing north. Not flying. Not making sound. Just waiting, with the patient collective attention of things that already know how this ends.

### Ambient Details

A few threads woven through the entire Wychfeld that reward attentive players:

The **Master's Footprints** — not literal footprints, but traces. A campfire ring at the Woodcutter's Chapel that is older than the current crisis. A carved mark on a Greywood tree that matches the style of the wizard's protective symbols on the oaks outside Thornwall Castle. A page torn from a journal, found in the abandoned merchant's cart, written in a hand the player won't be able to identify until the dungeon. The master walked this road. He left things behind. Following the traces forward is following him toward what happened to him.

The **Quality of Silence** changes as the player moves north. In the Amber Fields there are still birds — distant, rare, but present. In the Greywood they are gone but the wind remains. On the moor there is a low tonal hum just below the threshold of music. In the Ashenmoor Approach there is nothing — not quiet, but the active absence of sound, as if the landscape is holding something back.

The **Stars at Night** — if the player is still traveling after dark, the stars are wrong. Not dramatically, not in ways that suggest a different world. Just slightly wrong. A constellation that doesn't quite match any known pattern. A gap in the sky where stars should be and aren't, roughly north, roughly in the direction of Ashenmoor. It is never commented on. It is simply there.

### What the Wychfeld Is

The Wychfeld is not merely the space between two points. It is the game's argument that evil spreads outward from its source like water from a broken vessel — that Ashenmoor's darkness is not contained within Ashenmoor's walls but is seeping through the soil and the air and the behavior of animals into the world beyond. Thornwall is holding its breath. The Wychfeld is where the breath has already run out.

The player who walks the full road — clears the wolf-haunted fields, fights through the goblin camps, crosses the blighted moor, climbs the watchtower and sees the whole picture at once — arrives at Ashenmoor's outer ruins not as a stranger but as someone who understands, in their bones, what is at stake. The cold of the moat water, when it comes, will feel earned.

## Dungeon - **ASHENMOOR CASTLE** — The Seat of the Banished Lord

Ashenmoor was not always its name. The old maps call it something else — something the townspeople of Thornwall have quietly stopped saying aloud, the way you stop saying the name of the dead after a certain point. Ashenmoor is what it has become. The name comes from the grey, mineral-stained quality of its stonework, visible even at distance through the trees — a pale, ashen colour that seems less like stone and more like bone the longer you look at it.

From the outside, approaching through the ruins of its outer ward, it appears derelict. Windows dark. Battlements crumbling. The drawbridge perpetually raised. This is deliberate. The Banished Lord does not want visitors. What lives inside Ashenmoor wants to be left alone to its work, which is patient and methodical and nearly complete.

The castle is built on a slight rise above the surrounding landscape, which means the moat sits lower than the ground around it — fed by underground channels rather than surface water, which is why it runs cold and dark regardless of season, and why the water inside it moves in ways that do not correspond to any wind.

### Exterior Approach

**The Outer Ruins**
The outermost ring of Ashenmoor — a collapsed curtain wall and the remains of a gatehouse — have been abandoned long enough that trees have begun growing through the stonework. This is where the player first arrives and finds the castle inaccessible by conventional means. The ruins are not empty. Something has nested in the collapsed gatehouse — a shape that watches from the darkness inside the arch and does not move until the player gets too close. The ground here is soft and dark, the grass dead in concentric rings around the outer wall as if the earth itself is exhaling something toxic outward from the foundations.

A crow sits on every visible surface. None of them make sound.

**The Dark Pool**
Set into a hollow just north of the outer ruins, fed by the same underground channels as the moat, the pool is where the wraith is summoned. It is perfectly still regardless of weather. Its surface has a faint iridescence at night — not beautiful, exactly, but compelling in the way that dangerous things sometimes are. The bottom is not visible. Old scratch-marks on the stones at the waterline suggest something has climbed out of it many times before.

**The Moat**
Wide, cold, and lightless. The water is dark enough to be opaque from above but somehow luminous from within once the player is submerged — a faint green-grey phosphorescence from below that illuminates nothing usefully and makes the disorientation worse. The moat is not empty. Things drift in it — not aggressive, not quite present, more like suggestions of shapes. The wraith keeps them from interfering. Barely.

### The Barbican and Entry

**The Drawbridge**
Ancient oak reinforced with iron, lowered from inside by mechanisms the player cannot access. When the player emerges from the moat onto the embankment, the drawbridge is the only crossing. It is down — left down by the castle's inhabitants who have grown confident enough in their isolation to stop worrying about visitors. This confidence is their first mistake.

**The Barbican**
A narrow, vaulted passage through the gatehouse, wide enough for two men abreast. Two sentries stand here — not the corrupted demonic peasants of the courtyard but proper soldiers, or what used to be proper soldiers, their armor still bearing the old castle livery beneath the rust. They are quiet, methodical, and wrong in some way the player notices before they can name it — they move with slightly too much precision, blink slightly too infrequently. The Banished Lord's influence runs deepest in those who served here longest.

The barbican walls are carved with old protective inscriptions that have been deliberately defaced — chiseled out in long diagonal strokes, as one of the first acts of the new occupancy. Whatever they were warding against, the new residents wanted it let in.

### The Courtyard

Wide, irregular, paved in dark stone that has cracked and heaved over the years so that weeds push through in thick clumps. Several large fire-pits burn here at all hours — not for warmth, exactly, but as if maintaining the fires is itself the point. The light they cast is orange-red and unsteady, throwing long shadows that seem to move independently of anything casting them.

**The Demonic Peasants** mill around the fires with the aimless, looping quality of people who have forgotten why they're there. They were once villagers — from settlements further north, or from the castle's own surrounding farmland. They are not in pain. That is almost the worst thing about them. They hum to themselves, fragments of songs they no longer remember the words to, in a register slightly too low to be comfortable.

**The Well** in the courtyard centre is sealed with a heavy iron plate bolted down from the outside — something in the player's instinct will register this as significant and unsettling. Whatever is down there, someone above it wanted it to stay. The iron plate is warm to the touch.

**The Stable Block** along the eastern wall is full of horses that are no longer horses in any meaningful sense — large, dark, absolutely silent, facing the wall. The player can pass through it as a route to avoid part of the courtyard. The horses do not react to the player's presence. They simply stand. Facing the wall.

### The Great Hall

Long, high-ceilinged, hung with tapestries that depict the castle's original heraldry — a silver hawk on black — which someone has modified with dark pigment so that the hawk now clutches something in its talons that isn't a prey animal. Trestle tables run the length of the hall, set with food in various states of consumption and decay. Candles burn in iron chandeliers overhead.

**The Feast** is not a celebration. It is a ritual, though the participants may not know it. A dozen figures sit at the tables — soldiers, corrupted attendants, a steward still clutching his ledger even now — eating and drinking in the same looping, mechanical way the courtyard peasants mill around the fires. The food on the tables is real. This is perhaps the most disturbing detail in the hall — somebody cooked it. Somebody has been maintaining this pretense of domestic normalcy inside a castle that has been swallowed by darkness.

A **minstrel** sits in the corner, playing. The instrument is out of tune. The song has no recognizable melody. He has been playing it for a long time.

The hall's far end opens into a short corridor leading to the private chambers. The door at the end is the heaviest in the castle — iron-banded oak, carved with a single motif: a figure kneeling before a larger figure, both rendered in a style that makes it ambiguous whether the kneeling figure is worshipping or begging.

### The Private Chambers

The personal quarters of whoever ruled here before the fall, now occupied by the **Demonic Prince and Princess** — not royalty in any hereditary sense, but demonic nobility, granted the castle's finest rooms as a reward for their service. They have redecorated according to their own tastes, which run toward darkness and disorder — furniture overturned or rearranged into configurations that suggest ritual purposes, mirrors covered with black cloth, the windows sealed with tapestries pulled from the hall.

**The Prince** is elegant in the way that predators sometimes are — economical, precise, dressed in the previous occupant's finery with no apparent irony. He fights with a long, thin blade of black iron and a secondary weapon that is essentially concentrated spite given physical form — a bolt of dark energy he discharges at close range when cornered.

**The Princess** is more dangerous than she initially appears. She is the one casting. The Prince is her shield and her distraction. She will sacrifice him without hesitation if it creates an opening, and she will spend the entire fight making the player believe the opposite is true.

The chambers contain the best loot in the castle's upper half. They also contain, half-hidden behind a displaced wardrobe, **the hatchway** — iron ring set into the floor, hinges oiled recently, descending by narrow stone steps into a different quality of darkness entirely.

### The Dungeons

The air changes immediately on descent. Above, the castle smells of smoke and decay and corrupted humanity. Below, it smells of something older — stone and cold water and the particular quality of air that has not moved in centuries, now threaded through with something sharp and chemical, like the air before lightning.

**The Upper Dungeon** is cells and corridors, torchlit, occupied by things that were imprisoned here before the castle fell and have been left in their cells since — not freed, not killed, simply ignored by the new occupancy, which suggests either oversight or that something down here was judged too dangerous to release even by the Banished Lord.

The Latin begins here. Faint at first — a single voice, distant, the words blurring together. As the player descends further, additional voices join, layering into a choral drone that is not quite music and not quite prayer and has a physical quality in the chest.

**The Latin choir** is the player's navigation tool in the dungeon. The louder it grows, the deeper they are, the closer they are. It is also slowly wrong — starting with recognizable liturgical fragments and becoming, over the descent, something no liturgy ever contained.

**The Corridor of the Demonic Knights** — the final approach to the deepest cell — is a long, vaulted passage lit by fires burning in iron sconces that burn blue rather than orange. The knights stand at intervals along the walls, not patrolling, simply standing, fully armored, visors down. They wait until the player crosses an invisible threshold and then they all move at once. There are seven of them. The corridor is not wide. It is the hardest combat encounter in the game before the finale and it is designed to leave the player battered, low on resources, and acutely aware that whatever is behind the door at the end required this much protection.

### The Deepest Cell

The door is not locked. It opens inward and silently, on perfect hinges, as if it has been expecting this.

The room beyond is large — larger than seems architecturally possible given the dungeon's dimensions above, a detail the player registers subconsciously as wrong before anything else registers. The floor is inscribed with a vast geometric figure in what appears to be a combination of etched stone and dried substances the player does not look at too closely. Candles at the figure's vertices burn with the same blue as the corridor sconces.

At the far end, **the Master** stands with his back to the player, arms raised, reciting. The portal before him is not yet open — it is a seam in the air, a wrongness, a place where the room's geometry disagrees with itself, cycling slowly through colors that have no names. Beside the Master, the **Demonic Warlock** kneels in the geometric figure's centre, feeding it.

And at the warlock's shoulder, absolutely still, enormous, watching the door with the calm of something that has never needed to hurry about anything —

The **Demonic Knight Lord**.

The room smells of ozone and Latin and something the player's body recognizes before their mind does as the smell of a threshold — of a door between things that should not have a door between them being forced, slowly, open.

### The Architecture of Dread

What makes Ashenmoor work as a space is the sense that it was once *good* — that the hawk-on-black banner meant something honorable, that the great hall hosted genuine celebration, that the dungeon held genuine criminals rather than cosmological instruments. The evil here is not ancient and intrinsic but recent and applied, a corruption laid over something that had its own dignity.

The player destroys the castle at the end. The explosion that takes down the foundations and buries everything in rubble is also, in a real sense, a mercy — releasing a building from what it became, returning its stones to the earth, which neither knows nor cares what happened inside them.

The crows on the outer ruins will be gone when the dust settles. Nobody will notice for several days.
