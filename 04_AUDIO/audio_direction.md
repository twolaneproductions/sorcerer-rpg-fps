# audio_direction.md
## Ashenmoor — Audio Direction
**Gate Shaker Press**
**Version:** 0.1
**Last Updated:** 2026-04-26
**Status:** Phase 0 — Draft

---

## Philosophy

The audio of Ashenmoor tells the same story as the visuals — warmth becoming dread as the player moves north. Sound is not decoration. Every audio choice either communicates information, establishes atmosphere, or both. Nothing plays for its own sake.

Silence is used deliberately and sparingly. When the sound drops out, it means something. The player should feel the absence.

---

## Music

**Overall approach:** Sparse, textural, non-melodic for the most part. Music should feel like it belongs to the environment rather than being scored over it. The player should not always be certain whether they are hearing music or an ambient sound.

**Thornwall**
Warm, distant, human. A single instrument — lute, hurdy-gurdy, or recorder — heard from inside a tavern or across a courtyard. Never foregrounded. The music of a town that is still functioning and does not want to admit how frightened it is. The emotional register is *ordinary life under pressure*, not *heroic departure*.

**Wychfeld — Amber Fields**
The music stops when the player leaves Thornwall's gate. What replaces it is wind, distant crows, and a very low sustained tone — barely musical, more felt than heard. Occasional fragments of the Thornwall melody surface and dissolve before completing. The world is forgetting warmth.

**Wychfeld — Greywood**
The low tone deepens. Occasional percussive sounds — wood creaking, something moving in branches — that are rhythmic enough to suggest music without committing to it. The player's footsteps are louder here than they should be.

**Ashenmoor Exterior**
No music. Fire sounds, wind in wrong directions, the distant non-sound of the moat. The silence here is active — it is the sound of something that has consumed all the other sounds.

**Ashenmoor Interior**
The Latin begins.

## The Latin Choir

The game's signature audio element. A choral drone in archaic Latin that originates somewhere below the dungeon and grows as the player descends. It functions simultaneously as atmosphere, navigation tool, and escalating dread.

**Behavior by zone:**

| Zone | Latin Character |
|---|---|
| Barbican / Courtyard | Inaudible — silence only |
| Great Hall | Just audible — a single voice, words unclear, could be imagined |
| Chambers | Two or three voices, words occasionally distinguishable — recognizable liturgical fragments |
| Upper Dungeon | Full choir, words clear — legitimate Latin prayer, Gregorian-adjacent |
| Dungeon Corridor | Words beginning to shift — syllables that don't belong in the liturgy, rhythm wrong |
| Deepest Cell | No longer prayer — the Latin is correct in its sounds but the meaning has inverted. Something is being opened, not closed. |

**Production note:** The Latin shift from liturgy to inversion should be subtle enough that players who don't know Latin feel it emotionally before they understand it intellectually. Players who do know Latin should feel it as a specific horror. Both experiences are valid and intended.

## Ambient Sound Design

**Thornwall**
Layered human sounds — distant conversation, a hammer on an anvil, a dog's claws on cobblestone, fire in a hearth, rain on a roof. The ambient bed should feel like a town. Individual sounds should be identifiable but not isolated — everything bleeds into everything else the way real environments do.

**Wychfeld**
Wind with directional inconsistency — it should occasionally seem to come from the wrong direction. Bird sounds present in the Amber Fields, absent by the Greywood. The wolves are silent until close. The goblins are audible before visible — guttural, organized, purposeful.

**Ashenmoor Exterior**
Fire sounds dominant. Crows present but silent — their wingbeats are audible, their calls are not. The moat makes no sound, which is wrong for moving water and should register as wrong.

**Ashenmoor Interior**
Echo. Everything in the castle reverberates slightly longer than the architecture should allow. Footsteps, spells, combat — all carry a faint tail. The great hall's minstrel plays in the background during that encounter — out of tune, unrecognizable melody, continuous.

**The Dungeons**
The echo lengthens further. The player's own breathing becomes audible. The Latin choir occupies the frequency range where ambient sound normally lives — there is no ambient bed in the dungeon, only the choir and the player's sounds against it.

## Combat Audio

**Weapons**
Each wand and staff should sound distinct and readable — a player should know which weapon they are using without looking at the HUD.

| Weapon | Sound Character |
|---|---|
| Witch Switch | Dry crack, small, immediate |
| Scatterstaff | Wide percussive burst, bone-rattle texture |
| Tempest Rod | Wind-up whine building to sustained electrical crackle |
| Comet Staff | Deep thump on charge, resonant boom on detonation |
| Magestream Wand | Rapid high-register violet hiss |
| Obliterator Stave | Two-second silence during charge, then a sound that is more pressure than noise |
| Hungering Branch | Wet, organic, continuous — uncomfortable to listen to |

**Enemy sounds**
Corrupted humans should retain fragments of human sound — the demonic peasants hum in too-low registers, the soldiers' armor sounds normal but their breathing does not. The purely demonic enemies (Prince, Princess, Knights, Lord) are large-scale and resonant — sound design that communicates physical mass.

**The Knight Lord** makes almost no sound until the beheading. The beheading itself should be the loudest single sound in the game.

## The Finale Sequence

The finale's audio is the game's most carefully sequenced moment and should be treated as a separate production exercise.

**Beat sequence:**
1. Latin choir at full distortion as the portal approaches completion
2. Player enters the cell — choir cuts to near-silence on threshold crossing
3. The Master's incantation audible for the first time — his voice specifically, not choral
4. The Lord moves — large, slow footsteps, each one felt as much as heard
5. The beheading — loudest sound in the game, followed by absolute silence
6. Several seconds of silence — the head on the floor, nothing
7. A single syllable, barely voiced — the first incantation from the severed head
8. The body begins — blood sounds, then electrical crackle building
9. Each spell detonation distinct and escalating
10. The foundations — a deep structural groan building to collapse
11. Silence

**The final silence should last longer than is comfortable.**

## Voice Acting

Not in scope for initial release. All dialogue is text-based. This decision is revisited post-launch.

If voice acting is added in a future update, the casting direction is as follows: no performance should indicate genre awareness. Every character speaks as though their situation is real. King Orvyn sounds like a tired soldier, not a king from a fantasy game. The wraith sounds like a drowned man, not a ghost from a horror film.

---

## Music and Audio Sources

Given the minimal budget, the following approaches are being considered:

- Original composition by a collaborator to be identified
- Licensed medieval and early music recordings in public domain
- Custom ambient textures produced in-house using accessible tools

The Latin choir specifically should be original — no existing recording will have the precise behavioral arc the sequence requires. This is the one audio element where custom production is non-negotiable.
