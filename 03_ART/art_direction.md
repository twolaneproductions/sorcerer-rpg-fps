## Ashenmoor — Art Direction
**Gate Shaker Press**
**Version:** 0.1
**Last Updated:** 2026-04-26
**Status:** Phase 0 — Draft

---

## Visual Identity

Thornwall should look like a place worth saving. The Wychfeld should look like a place in the process of being lost. Ashenmoor should look like a place that has already been lost and does not know it yet.

The tone should never get too heavy, and that should be reflected in the art style -- never too realistic, too grotesque, too morbid.

---

## Color Palette

The game's color temperature shifts as the player moves north. This is the primary visual argument of the overworld journey.

| Zone | Dominant Colors | Light Quality |

| Ashenmoor — Exterior | Ashen grey, rust orange firelight, bone white | Fire in wrong colors |
| Ashenmoor — Interior | Deep shadow, blue-black, cold torch blue | Minimal, oppressive |
| Ashenmoor — Dungeons | Near black, veins of bioluminescent cold green | Deep, suffocating, alive |

**No pure black and no pure white anywhere in the game.** Shadows have color. Light has temperature.

## Environment Design

**Thornwall**
Stone foundations, timber upper stories, thatched or slate roofs. Worn but maintained — the wear is the wear of use, not neglect. Every building has been touched by human hands recently. Firelight in windows. Laundry. Boot prints in mud. The town should feel inhabited down to its details.

**Thornwall Castle**
Larger scale than the town, same material vocabulary. The distinction is stone throughout — no timber. Banners are genuine fabric, worn at the edges. The great hall map table has actual paper maps with handwritten notations. The throne is oak and iron, not gilded. Power here is functional, not decorative.

**The Wychfeld**
The farmland transition is abrupt and deliberate — green to grey within a few steps of the town gate. Dead crops still standing in rows. Farmhouses structurally intact, details wrong. The Greywood's trees lean slightly toward the road. The light through the canopy is greenish and slightly too uniform. Nothing dramatic. Just persistently wrong.

**Ashenmoor**
The castle is built from stone with a faint mineral greyness that reads as pale rather than white — ashen, as named. Battlements are intact but unmaintained. The courtyard fires burn in iron pits and cast orange-red light with too many shadows for the number of sources. The interior goes darker with each zone — hall to chambers to dungeon, a deliberate compression of light.

The dungeon's bioluminescent blue-green is the only color below-ground. It should be beautiful in the way dangerous things are beautiful — present without being welcoming.

## Character and Enemy Design

**Player (Sorcerer)**
Apprentice-scholar aesthetic. Dark wool robes, plain leather belt, soft boots. No armor that reads as warrior gear. The wands and staves are the most visually interesting things about him — carved wood, crystal fittings, wrapped grips. As armor is acquired the robes are supplemented, not replaced. The Wizard's Own Amulet should be the most visually distinctive piece he wears.

**Townspeople**
Period-appropriate working clothes. Muted colors — madder red, woad blue, undyed linen. Nobody is wearing anything they couldn't have made or traded locally. The hedge-witch Maret is the single exception — her seven rings and ink-stained fingers are her visual signature.

**King Orvyn**
Functional authority. A surcoat over practical clothing, a plain crown that reads as a working tool rather than a symbol. He should look like a soldier who became a king rather than the other way around.

**Enemies**
Corrupted humans retain their original visual identity beneath the corruption — the soldiers still wear the old castle livery, the peasants still wear working clothes. The corruption is in their movement, their eyes, their wrongness of posture, not in monstrous transformation. The demonic enemies (Prince, Princess, Knights, Lord) are more overtly supernatural but still wear the visual vocabulary of the castle — armored, dressed, formal. The Lord specifically should be enormous and still.

## Weapon Design

Wands and staves read as **instruments, not weapons** — carved and specific, like objects made by a craftsman for a purpose. Each has a visual identity that communicates its function:

- **Witch Switch** — bare, uncarved, almost accidental. A stick that happens to work.
- **Scatterstaff** — wide-mouthed, gnarled, organic. Bone-colored.
- **Tempest Rod** — gyroscopic, geometric, with moving parts that spin up before firing.
- **Comet Staff** — long, smooth, with a bright point that glows brighter as it charges.
- **Magestream Wand** — slender, precise, violet-tinted crystal fittings.
- **Obliterator Stave** — massive, two-handed, obsidian, with a pulsing green eye at the crown. The most overtly dangerous-looking object in the game.
- **Hungering Branch** — living wood, thorned, slightly writhing. The only weapon that looks alive.

## UI Design

The HUD is minimal and period-adjacent — no digital readouts, no floating numbers. Health is represented by a candle or flame indicator. Mana by an ink-fill or hourglass. Active consumable shown as a small item icon. The inventory screen uses a parchment or leather aesthetic — hand-lettered labels, no serif fonts that read as modern.

**The UI should look like it belongs to the same world as the game, not imported from a different aesthetic.**

## Reference Touchstones

These are directional, not prescriptive. The goal is the feeling, not the imitation:

- *Thief: The Dark Project* — environment texture and light philosophy
- *Dark Souls* — enemy design language, atmosphere without explanation
- *Heretic / Hexen* — fantasy FPS color palette precedent
- Medieval manuscript illumination — UI aesthetic and iconographic vocabulary
- Andrew Wyeth — color temperature and the feeling of a landscape that has gone wrong
